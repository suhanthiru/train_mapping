What is Train Tracker?

The whole project in plain words. ← back to hub · technical architecture →

The one-sentence version

It watches every NYC subway train live, predicts when each train will really arrive (often better than the official feed), learns from its own mistakes automatically, and now also notices when something abnormal is happening — and can usually say why.

How it works, step by step

  1. Listen. Every 30 seconds it pulls the MTA's live feed: where every train is and the MTA's own arrival guesses.
  2. Smooth. Positions between updates are estimated by a small Rust service (a Kalman filter) — that's why trains glide on the map instead of jumping, and it also tells us how far through its current hop a train is.
  3. Remember. Everything gets written down: what was predicted (by the MTA feed and by our own models), and what actually happened. That notebook is the ledger — 320k+ station-to-station trips so far.
  4. Learn. Twice a day-ish (every 6 hours), the models retrain on the grown ledger and hot-swap themselves in with no downtime. Two models: v1 answers "how long does this stretch of track take?", v2 answers "this train is 40% through the stretch — how many seconds are left?"
  5. Compare honestly. Every prediction source — the MTA feed, v1, v2 — is graded against reality on the same trips. The dashboard shows who's winning at every lead time. No cherry-picking.

What's new (July 2026)

Historical pretraining. The models used to know only what the ledger had personally seen — so rare track segments got bad guesses. We now also feed them seven years of official MTA running-time history (2019+), carefully down-weighted so real observations always win. Measured result: on segments the ledger had never seen, error dropped from ~228s to ~180s (−21%). A gate script (eval_pretrain.py) proves the blend wins before it's allowed to ship.

Anomaly detection — now visible. The system knows what "normal" looks like for every trip pattern — the scheduled time between stops, and the historical 50th/75th-percentile running times by day and time period. Every 30 seconds, each in-progress train is checked against that baseline; one running well past it gets flagged, cross-referenced against active service alerts, and tagged with the most likely cause for its line (e.g. "Police & Medical" vs "Infrastructure"). Flagged trains pulse amber on the live map, appear in the dashboard's Anomalies panel with a click-through to the map, and every episode is saved for later review. A per-route ops row fuses anomalies + alerts + model accuracy — the first slice of the integrated ops view. This is the piece that catches the big 3-5 minute disruptions that no average-based model can predict.

One-command startup. npm run dev:all starts all five services together with joined logs; the service hub shows live green/red dots for each and links everywhere. No more five terminals and five memorized ports.

Why two models instead of one?

v1 thinks in whole track segments, so when a train is already most of the way through one, v1 still charges the full time — that made it systematically late. v2 fixes that by using the live Kalman position ("you're 70% through, ~50 seconds left"). Near-term, v2's error is ~30 seconds. The official feed is still sharper in the final minute; our models win on longer horizons and on segments the feed handles poorly. There's also an experimental graph neural network that models how trains delay each other — it's kept strictly offline until it proves itself.

The July 2026 roadmap — all six phases done

  • Quick wins ✓ pretraining locked into production, anomaly checks ~2000× faster, memory leak + three slow queries fixed.
  • Safety net ✓ first tests + CI (they immediately caught a real bug in the model-promotion gate), pinned dependencies, container healthchecks.
  • Cleanup ✓ one feature-encoder, one data-download client, one alert index (was 4 disagreeing variants), one config source for every port/color, and a circuit-breaker on the model bridge.
  • Anomaly dashboard ✓ live panel + per-route ops row + amber map pulses — verified against real trains during a genuine system-wide slowdown (39 flagged, 33 corroborated by active alerts).
  • Deep optimizations ✓ live-map traffic cut 77% (131→36 MB/hour per viewer), history writes cut 41% with playback verified identical, and a feature registry so new model features touch one JSON instead of five files.

Naturally next: richer ops fusion (headway + schedule anomalies in one timeline), incident post-mortems from the anomaly history, and promoting the GNN if its offline experiment clears the gate.
